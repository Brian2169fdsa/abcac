# Certification vs. Licensure in Arizona: The One Distinction Every Addiction Counselor Must Understand

If you're building a career in addiction counseling in Arizona, there's a single point of confusion that trips up more new professionals than any other: **certification and licensure are not the same thing.** They're issued by different organizations, they mean different things legally, and mixing them up can cost you a job offer, a billable service, or months of wasted effort.

Let's clear it up for good.

## The short version

- **Licensure** comes from the **Arizona Board of Behavioral Health Examiners (AZBBHE)**. It is the **legal authority** to practice, diagnose, and bill for behavioral health services in Arizona. If you want to work independently, this is not optional.
- **Certification** comes from the **Arizona Board for Certification of Addiction Counselors (ABCAC)**, an **IC&RC member board**. It's a **voluntary professional credential** focused on substance use counseling that's recognized across state lines through IC&RC's reciprocity network.

ABCAC itself puts it plainly: its certification is a voluntary credential recognized internationally through IC&RC, while AZBBHE licensure is a state-issued legal requirement for independent practice — the thing that actually lets you diagnose, treat, and bill for services in Arizona.

One is a legal license. The other is professional recognition. You can hold both, and many counselors do.

## What licensure (AZBBHE) actually gives you

The AZBBHE license is what the state of Arizona checks before you can independently assess, diagnose, treat, and bill. Arizona's addiction-counseling license ladder has three rungs:

| License | Level | Typical education | Practice authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LACT** (Licensed Addiction [Counselor] Technician) | Entry | Associate in addiction / bachelor's in a behavioral science | Dependent, supervised |
| **LAAC** (Licensed Associate Addiction Counselor) | Associate | Bachelor's + supervised experience | Supervised |
| **LIAC** (Licensed Independent Addiction Counselor) | Independent | Master's or higher + supervised hours | Independent — can diagnose and supervise |

> **Heads-up on titles:** If you earned your license before late 2024, you may know these as LSAT, LASAC, and LISAC. Arizona renamed them under SB1062, effective September 14, 2024. Same board, new names — and a broader scope that now includes behavioral (process) addictions.

Licensure requires board-approved education, supervised experience, and a passing score on an approved exam (Arizona accepts IC&RC, NAADAC, or NBCC exams depending on your discipline and level). It also carries ongoing obligations: every renewal requires Arizona's Statutes & Regulations Tutorial plus continuing education in ethics, behavioral health law, and cultural competency.

## What certification (ABCAC) actually gives you

ABCAC certification is a **credential**, not a license. It signals to employers, payers, and peers that you've met a nationally benchmarked standard in substance use counseling. ABCAC's credentials include:

- **CAC** — Certified Addiction Counselor (entry-level)
- **CADAC** — Certified Alcohol & Drug Abuse Counselor (bachelor's level)
- **AADC** — Advanced Alcohol & Drug Counselor (master's level)
- Plus specialty credentials: **CCS** (Clinical Supervisor), **CCJP** (Criminal Justice Professional), **CPS** (Prevention Specialist), and **CPRS** (Peer Recovery Support Specialist)

The biggest practical advantage of ABCAC certification is **portability**. Because ABCAC is an IC&RC member board, your reciprocal-level credential (CADAC and above) can transfer to other IC&RC states without starting from scratch. One important caveat: the entry-level **CAC is not eligible for IC&RC reciprocity** — only CADAC, AADC, and the reciprocal specialty credentials are.

## So which one do you actually need?

Here's the honest answer: **it depends on what you want to do.**

- **Want to independently diagnose, treat, and bill insurance in Arizona?** You need an AZBBHE license. Certification alone won't authorize it.
- **Work at an agency that prefers or requires certified counselors?** You'll likely need ABCAC certification, and many Arizona treatment providers ask for it.
- **Plan to move between states during your career?** ABCAC certification with an IC&RC reciprocal credential makes that far smoother.
- **Want the strongest possible profile?** Hold both. The license makes you legal; the certification makes you portable and marketable.

The two systems also differ in the fine print. AZBBHE generally requires university-based classroom instruction for its education hours, while ABCAC accepts approved distance and self-study education. That difference matters when you're planning coursework, so map your target credential *before* you enroll in anything.

## The takeaway

Think of it this way: **licensure is permission; certification is proof.** Arizona law cares about the license. Employers, payers, and other states increasingly care about the certification. Knowing which is which — and which you need for the career you're building — is the first real step toward doing this work well in Arizona.
