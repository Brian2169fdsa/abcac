# Inside the IC&RC ADC Exam: Format, Scoring, and the 4 Domains

The IC&RC Alcohol and Drug Counselor (**ADC**) exam is the gateway to a reciprocal-level addiction credential in Arizona and 46 other states. It's the exam behind ABCAC's CADAC certification and one of the exams Arizona accepts for licensure. If you're preparing to sit for it, the worst thing you can do is walk in without understanding how it's built. Let's fix that.

## The format at a glance

The ADC exam is a computer-based, multiple-choice test:

- **150 total questions** — but only **125 are scored**. The other **25 are unscored "pretest" items** that IC&RC is trialing for future exams. You won't know which are which, so you treat every question as if it counts.
- **3-hour time limit.** That works out to roughly **72 seconds per question** if you use the full window — comfortable, but not unlimited.
- **Delivered through Prometric** (with some administrations via ISO-Quality Testing), either in person at a testing center or through a remote-proctored option.

## How scoring actually works

This is where a lot of candidates get confused, so read carefully.

- Your score is reported on a **scaled range of 200 to 800.**
- The **passing score is 500.**
- That **500 is a scaled score, not a raw percentage.** You are *not* being told "get 70% right to pass."

IC&RC sets the passing standard using a method called **Modified Angoff**, in which subject-matter experts judge how a minimally competent counselor would perform on each item. The raw number of questions you need varies slightly from form to form, which is why IC&RC reports a scaled score rather than a percentage. Practically, that means the exact number of correct answers required can shift, but the standard of competence stays constant.

One piece of good news: **there's no penalty for guessing.** Every question left blank is a guaranteed zero, so you answer all of them — even the ones you're unsure about.

## The four domains (and their weights)

The ADC exam is built on four **performance domains**, each carrying a different weight. This weighting is the single most useful thing to know when you plan your studying, because it tells you where the points actually are:

| Domain | Weight | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Scientific Principles of Substance Use & Co-occurring Disorders** | **25%** | Pharmacology, the neuroscience of addiction, co-occurring mental health conditions |
| **2. Evidence-Based Screening & Assessment** | **20%** | Screening tools, assessment, diagnosis, treatment planning foundations |
| **3. Evidence-Based Treatment, Counseling & Referral** | **30%** | Counseling techniques, treatment modalities, case management, referral |
| **4. Professional, Ethical & Legal Responsibilities** | **25%** | Ethics, confidentiality, documentation, professional conduct |

Notice that **Domain 3 is the single largest chunk at 30%.** Counseling and treatment is where the most points live — which should shape how you allocate your study time.

Also notice that **ethics and confidentiality run through the whole exam.** Domain 4 alone is 25%, but confidentiality concepts — especially federal SUD-records law under **42 CFR Part 2** — surface across multiple domains. Studying Part 2 thoroughly pays off in more places than you'd expect.

## What kind of counselor passes?

IC&RC doesn't publish an official pass rate, but third-party prep providers commonly estimate the ADC first-time pass rate in the **60–70% range.** That's a meaningful failure rate — this is not a rubber-stamp exam. The candidates who pass tend to share a few habits:

- They studied the **official IC&RC ADC Candidate Guide** and used its content outline as a checklist.
- They took **full-length practice exams** under timed conditions, not just flashcards.
- They spent proportionally more time on **Domain 3** and made sure they were solid on **ethics and confidentiality.**

## If you don't pass the first time

It happens to plenty of competent counselors. IC&RC's retake rules:

- There's a **mandatory 90-day waiting period** between attempts. Use it — don't just rebook and re-guess.
- After **four consecutive failed attempts**, you're required to complete remedial action before testing again.

A failed attempt is information, not a verdict. Candidates who fail once and then study *to their weak domain* usually pass on the retake.

## Test-day essentials

- Confirm whether you're testing **in person or remotely** and prepare accordingly — remote proctoring (ProProctor) has its own equipment and environment requirements.
- Bring the identification your testing confirmation specifies.
- Pace yourself against that ~72-seconds-per-question benchmark, and **answer every question** — remember, no guessing penalty.

## The takeaway

The ADC exam is 150 questions (125 scored), scored 200–800, with a passing line of 500 set by expert judgment rather than a fixed percentage. Four domains carry different weights, and Domain 3 — treatment and counseling — is the biggest at 30%. Study to the weights, drill your ethics and confidentiality, and take timed practice exams. Do that, and you'll walk in knowing exactly what you're facing.

### Studying for the ADC?

Start with the free official **IC&RC ADC Candidate Guide** and use its content outline to structure your prep. Fees, scheduling, and remote-proctoring options for Arizona candidates should be confirmed directly with **ABCAC**, since exam logistics and pricing change.

*This article reflects the ADC exam blueprint in effect as of early 2026. IC&RC updates its exams periodically; confirm the current format and content outline at internationalcredentialing.org before your exam date.*
