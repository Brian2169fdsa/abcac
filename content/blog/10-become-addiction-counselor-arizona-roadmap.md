# Getting Started as an Addiction Counselor in Arizona: The Complete 2026 Roadmap

Arizona needs addiction counselors — badly. The state is facing rising overdose deaths, a stubborn workforce shortage, and growing demand for behavioral health services. If you're thinking about entering this field, you're stepping into work that matters and a job market that wants you.

But the path can look confusing from the outside, largely because of one thing beginners almost always mix up: **licensure and certification are two different systems.** This roadmap walks you through both, in order, so you know exactly what to do first.

## Step 0: Understand the two tracks

Before anything else, internalize this:

- **Licensure** (from the **Arizona Board of Behavioral Health Examiners, AZBBHE**) is the **legal authorization** to practice, diagnose, and bill in Arizona. To work independently, you need it.
- **Certification** (from the **Arizona Board for Certification of Addiction Counselors, ABCAC**, an IC&RC member board) is a **voluntary, portable credential** that employers value and that transfers across state lines.

You may pursue one, the other, or both. Most serious addiction-counseling careers involve licensure at minimum. Keep this distinction in your head as you read the rest.

## Step 1: Get the right education

Your degree determines where you start on the licensure ladder:

- **Associate degree** in addiction / behavioral health with a counseling emphasis → points toward the entry **LACT** license.
- **Bachelor's degree** in a behavioral science → points toward the **LAAC** (associate) license.
- **Master's degree or higher** → the path to the independent **LIAC** license.

A few practical notes:

- For **licensure**, AZBBHE generally requires **university-based classroom instruction** for its education hours. For **certification**, ABCAC accepts approved distance and self-study education. That difference can shape which programs you choose.
- Look for programs with recognized accreditation (for example, NASAC-accredited addiction-studies programs or regionally accredited institutions), which tend to align well with credential requirements.
- Plan your coursework against your *target* credential's specific education hours so nothing is wasted.

## Step 2: Accumulate supervised experience

Both tracks require supervised, hands-on experience — and this is where a lot of your timeline lives.

- For **licensure**, each rung has its own experience requirement. The independent **LIAC**, for example, requires at least **1,600 hours of direct client contact over a minimum of 24 months** (no more than 400 in psychoeducation), plus a supervisor's competency attestation.
- For **ABCAC certification**, the hour requirements vary by credential — for instance, the bachelor's-level CADAC calls for substantial supervised experience in the IC&RC domains.

The universal rule: **make your hours count.** Confirm before you start that your supervisor is qualified and that the hours you're logging satisfy the requirements for the credential you actually want. Keep meticulous records — hour logs and supervisor attestations are exactly what the boards review.

### A shortcut for new grads: HB2001

Arizona created a helpful on-ramp. Under **HB2001** (effective September 26, 2025), qualifying recent graduates can practice under supervision for a **90-day window** while their associate-level license application is pending — and those hours count toward licensure. That means you may be able to start earning supervised experience (and a paycheck) sooner than you'd expect. Confirm your eligibility, but don't overlook it.

## Step 3: Pass your exam

For addiction-counseling **licensure**, AZBBHE accepts a board-approved exam — the **IC&RC** exam (ADC or AADC), a **NAADAC** exam, or an **NBCC** exam, depending on your level and discipline.

Here's a strategic tip: if you want **both** licensure and ABCAC certification, choosing the **IC&RC** exam can let a single exam serve both goals, since ABCAC is an IC&RC member board. Plan your exam choice around your full set of credential goals, not just the immediate one.

The most common entry exam, the IC&RC **ADC**, is 150 questions (125 scored), scored 200–800 with a passing line of 500, built on four weighted domains. It's a real exam with a meaningful failure rate — study to the domain weights and use the official IC&RC candidate guide.

## Step 4: Apply for your license and/or certification

- **Licensure:** Submit your application to **AZBBHE** with your transcripts, supervised-hour documentation, and exam results. Remember that licensure carries ongoing obligations — Arizona's Statutes & Regulations Tutorial plus ethics/law/cultural-competency CE at each renewal.
- **Certification:** Apply to **ABCAC** for the credential that matches your level (CAC, CADAC, AADC, or a specialty credential). Certification renews every two years and requires continuing education (for most credentials, 40 clock hours every two years, including ethics and cultural-diversity hours).

> **On ABCAC fees:** Published fees for ABCAC application, certification, and reciprocity should be confirmed **directly with ABCAC** — the current figures are best verified at the source before you budget.

## Step 5: Land your first job

The good news: Arizona's workforce shortage means demand for qualified addiction counselors is strong, and the field is projected to keep growing. As you job-hunt:

- **Target settings that match your credential level.** Entry roles often welcome LACT/LAAC-level counselors under supervision; independent roles want the LIAC.
- **Highlight both your license and your certification.** Many Arizona treatment providers specifically value ABCAC certification.
- **Consider where the need is greatest** — rural and tribal communities, and programs funded by Arizona's opioid-settlement dollars, are actively building capacity.

Set expectations on pay realistically for the entry phase (Arizona's median wage for substance-use, behavioral-disorder, and mental-health counselors was around **$49,920** as of May 2024), and know that advancing up the license ladder and adding certifications increases both your authority and your earning potential.

## Your roadmap at a glance

1. **Understand the two tracks** — licensure (required to practice) vs. certification (voluntary, portable).
2. **Get the right education** for your target credential.
3. **Accumulate supervised hours** that actually count — and use HB2001 if you qualify.
4. **Pass your exam** — choose IC&RC if you want licensure and certification to share one exam.
5. **Apply** to AZBBHE (license) and/or ABCAC (certification).
6. **Land a job** in a field that genuinely needs you.

## The takeaway

Becoming an addiction counselor in Arizona is a clear, achievable path once you separate the two systems: get your license from AZBBHE to practice legally, and add ABCAC/IC&RC certification to strengthen and mobilize your career. Match your education and hours to your target credential, choose your exam strategically, and take advantage of on-ramps like HB2001. Arizona is short on counselors and long on need — there's real room here for people ready to do the work.
